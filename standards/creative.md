# Creative Brief Standard

> The Davis Elen Creative Brief is the strategic foundation for all creative work. A strong brief ensures alignment between the client's objectives and the creative team's direction. This standard defines what a DE creative brief includes and how it should be structured.

## Purpose

The creative brief translates business objectives and strategic thinking into creative direction. It's:

- A communication tool between strategy and creative teams
- A reference document for keeping work on track
- A way to evaluate whether creative work is strategically sound
- Your defense against scope creep and misalignment

**A strong brief prevents rework. A weak brief ensures it.**

---

## The Brief at a Glance

A Davis Elen Creative Brief includes:

1. **Project Overview** — What are we making, and why?
2. **Business Objective** — What does the client want to achieve?
3. **Strategic Insight** — What deep truth about the audience does our strategy rely on?
4. **Target Audience** — Who are we talking to?
5. **Key Message** — What's the single most important thing we need to communicate?
6. **Tone & Personality** — How should our work sound and feel?
7. **Creative Direction** — What's our strategic approach to solving this challenge?
8. **Deliverables** — What are we making, and what are the specs?
9. **Success Criteria** — How will we measure if this work succeeded?
10. **Constraints & Guardrails** — What do we need to know or avoid?

**Length:** 1-3 pages (rarely longer)

---

## The Creative Brief Template

### PROJECT OVERVIEW

**Project Name:** [Campaign name or project title]

**Client:** [Client name]

**Project Type:** [Advertising campaign, website, brand identity, social media campaign, integrated campaign, etc.]

**Timeline:** [Start date → Delivery date]

**Disciplines Involved:** [Creative, media, digital, production, PR, etc.]

**Budget:** [If known and relevant to creative work]

---

### BUSINESS OBJECTIVE

**What does the client want to achieve with this work?**

State this in clear, business terms. Not creative terms. Not "make it pop." Business terms.

Examples:
- Increase store traffic by 15% during the holiday season
- Drive 500 qualified leads per month to the website
- Increase brand awareness among 25-34 year-old professionals by 25%
- Move McDonald's from "cheap and quick" to "quality and convenient"
- Re-position Smart & Final as the small business owner's supply partner

This should be one sentence. If it's more than one sentence, it's not clear enough.

---

### STRATEGIC INSIGHT

**What deep truth about the audience does our strategy rely on?**

This is the "why" behind the strategic direction. It's an insight into consumer behavior, motivations, or perceptions that makes our creative approach make sense.

This should feel true. It should be something the audience would recognize in themselves, even if they've never articulated it.

Examples:

- "Parents don't want to feel judged for buying convenient food. They want permission to be 'good enough.'"
- "Small business owners feel invisible to big suppliers. They want to be recognized as important customers."
- "Young professionals are ambitious but also skeptical of corporate messaging. They respond to authenticity and humor."

This insight should inform every creative decision you make. If you can't trace your creative choices back to this insight, your brief isn't clear enough.

**Note:** The strategic insight isn't always about the product or service. It's often about the audience's worldview, values, or behaviors.

---

### TARGET AUDIENCE

**Who are we talking to?**

Be specific. Not "people who like cars." Specific.

**Demographics:**
- Age range
- Gender (if relevant)
- Income / socioeconomic status (if relevant)
- Geography (if relevant)
- Occupation / role (if relevant)

**Psychographics / Attitudes:**
- What do they value?
- What are they worried about?
- What gets them excited?
- How do they see themselves?

**Behaviors:**
- How do they discover brands / products?
- What channels do they use?
- What influences their decisions?
- What's their relationship to this category (heavy user, light user, non-user)?

**Example:**

*Target: Busy parents in suburban areas, ages 30-45, with household income $75K-$150K, who have 2+ kids and work full-time.*

*They see themselves as good parents, but they're time-pressed and sometimes feel guilty about not cooking from scratch. They want their families to eat well, but they also value convenience and affordability. They're skeptical of "natural" or "organic" claims that seem overpriced. They trust recommendations from other parents and make decisions quickly (they don't have time to overthink).*

---

### KEY MESSAGE

**What's the single most important thing we need to communicate?**

Not the headline. Not the tagline. The core idea.

This should be expressible in plain English, in one sentence, in a way your mom would understand.

Examples:

- "McDonald's offers quality food without the wait"
- "We understand what small business owners need"
- "You can be a good parent and also take the convenient route"

Everything in your creative should support or illuminate this message. If you create something that doesn't connect to this message, you're off-brief.

**Test:** If someone saw your creative and someone else asked them what the message was, would they say something close to this?

---

### TONE & PERSONALITY

**How should our work sound and feel?**

This applies to everything: headlines, body copy, design, imagery, music, etc.

Define the tone with:
- **Adjectives:** (authoritative, playful, warm, edgy, sophisticated, approachable, etc.)
- **What we are:** (what tone/personality we're adopting)
- **What we're NOT:** (equally important—what tone we're avoiding)
- **Examples of how this sounds:** (sentence samples, or comparable brands/campaigns)

Example:

*Tone: Warm, authentic, slightly irreverent. Like a friend who gets it—who knows that good parenting isn't about perfection. Approachable but not condescending.*

*We are NOT: Preachy, sanctimonious, corporate, overly salesy.*

*How this sounds: "Real food. Real quick. That's enough."*

---

### CREATIVE DIRECTION

**What's our strategic approach to solving this challenge?**

This is where you describe the overarching creative strategy. Not specific ads, but the direction and approach.

This might include:

- **The strategic approach:** What's our big idea or frame for this campaign?
- **Emotional territory:** What emotional response are we going for? (humor, relief, inspiration, recognition, confidence, etc.)
- **Key visual or conceptual territory:** What world do we live in? What's the aesthetic or conceptual frame?
- **How we'll demonstrate the message:** What will we show or talk about to prove our point?

Example:

*We'll show real parents in real moments of convenience—picking up takeout, using a frozen meal, letting the kids eat in the car. Instead of shame or defensiveness, we'll celebrate these moments as proof that good parenting is about balance, not perfection. The tone is warm and recognition-based: "Yeah, that's us." The visual world is real life, not idealized—actual homes, actual kids, actual moments.*

---

### DELIVERABLES

**What are we making, and what are the specifications?**

Be specific:

- **Creative formats:** TV spots (how many, how long?), digital banner ads (what sizes?), social media content (what formats, how many posts?), print ads, radio spots, website pages, etc.
- **Specifications:** Video length, image dimensions, word counts, design specs, etc.
- **Timeline:** When is each deliverable due?
- **Approvals:** Who approves what, and when?

Example:

*Deliverables:*
- *1 x 30-second TV spot (edit for 15-second and 6-second versions)*
- *2 x Digital banner sets (300x250, 728x90, 970x250)*
- *Social media content: 8 posts over 4 weeks (mix of still images and short video)*
- *1 x Print full-page ad*
- *All assets delivered as files + brand-safe guidelines*

*Timeline:*
- *Initial concepts: [date]*
- *Client feedback received: [date]*
- *Final delivered: [date]*

---

### SUCCESS CRITERIA

**How will we know if this work succeeded?**

This should tie back to the business objective. What metrics matter?

Examples:

- Increase in store traffic during campaign period
- Website click-through rate or conversion rate
- Engagement metrics on social media
- Brand lift (pre/post survey)
- Sales lift in the category
- Qualified leads generated

**Note:** Some metrics are in your control (creative appeal, engagement), and some aren't (sales outcomes). Be clear about which is which, and how long to measure.

---

### CONSTRAINTS & GUARDRAILS

**What do we need to know or avoid?**

This is the fine print that prevents scope creep and keeps creative on track.

Examples:

- Brand guidelines or restrictions (logo size, color palette, imagery style, etc.)
- Legal or compliance requirements (disclaimers, regulatory approvals)
- Budget or resource constraints (no live action, animation only, limited production budget, etc.)
- Competitive considerations (can't mention competitors directly, etc.)
- Internal politics or sensitivities (CEO doesn't like the color blue, the product manager has strong opinions about messaging, etc.)
- Timing constraints (must launch before competitor announcement, must work with existing brand assets, etc.)

Be honest about these. They're not obstacles—they're reality.

## Brief Development Process

### Who Writes the Brief?

**Strategy Lead** owns the brief, but it's a collaborative document:

- **Account Lead** brings client context and objectives
- **Strategy Lead** synthesizes insights and writes the strategic thinking
- **Creative Director** ensures the brief is actionable and creatively inspiring
- **Media Director** (if relevant) ensures the brief aligns with media strategy
- **Client** reviews and approves (or refines) the brief before creative work begins

### When is the Brief Locked?

The brief should be locked and approved by:

1. The DE team (internal alignment)
2. The client (client alignment)

**Before** creative work begins. Not during. Before.

If creative is already underway and the brief changes, that's a scope change. Treat it as such.

### Common Mistakes in Brief-Writing

❌ **Too long.** If your brief is more than 3 pages, you don't have a brief yet—you have a report. Tighten it.

❌ **Too much creative direction.** The brief describes the direction; it doesn't prescribe the specific idea. Leave room for the creative team to think.

❌ **Vague business objective.** "Build brand awareness" is not an objective—it's a tactic. What are you trying to move? By how much?

❌ **Unclear target audience.** "Moms" is not a target. "Working parents in suburban areas, ages 30-45, with $75K-$150K household income" is a target.

❌ **No strategic insight.** The brief reads like a wish list instead of an insight. A good brief makes creative choices feel inevitable.

❌ **Copy written in the brief.** The brief isn't where you write headlines or taglines. Those live in the creative. The brief describes the strategy.

❌ **No success criteria.** How will you know this worked? If you can't answer that, the brief isn't ready.

---

## The Brief as a Living Document

The brief isn't set in stone, but changes should be intentional:

- **Pre-creative:** Brief gets refined based on client feedback or new information. This is normal.
- **During creative development:** The brief shouldn't change. If it needs to, that's a scope change.
- **Post-campaign:** The brief becomes a reference for evaluating performance. Did our strategy work?

---

## Quality Checklist: Is This a Strong Brief?

Before presenting creative, review the brief against this checklist:

- ✅ **One sentence business objective.** If you can't say it in one sentence, it's not clear enough.
- ✅ **Strategic insight feels true.** Does it feel like something the audience would recognize in themselves?
- ✅ **Target audience is specific.** Could someone unfamiliar with the brand understand exactly who we're talking to?
- ✅ **Key message is simple.** Would a 10-year-old understand it?
- ✅ **Tone is distinct.** Could you tell this work apart from competitors based on tone alone?
- ✅ **Creative direction is inspiring.** Does it make you want to make this work? Does it suggest creative possibilities?
- ✅ **Deliverables are clear.** Could someone build a production schedule from this?
- ✅ **Success criteria are measurable.** Could you look at the results and know if you succeeded?
- ✅ **Constraints are documented.** Has the team read this and understood the guardrails?
- ✅ **Brief is 1-3 pages.** If it's longer, edit it down.

If you checked all boxes, you're ready for creative work.
