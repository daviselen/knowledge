# Client Relationship Playbook

> Davis Elen's mission emphasizes partnership and long-term relationships. This playbook defines how we build, maintain, and strengthen client partnerships from kickoff through ongoing collaboration.

## Our Philosophy

Long-term relationships are built on:

- **Clear communication.** Clients always know what to expect, when to expect it, and how decisions are made.
- **Consistent delivery.** We do what we say we'll do, when we say we'll do it.
- **Proactive collaboration.** We don't just execute what clients ask for—we think strategically about their business and bring ideas to the table.
- **Honest feedback.** We tell clients what we think, even when it's not what they want to hear.
- **Respect for their constraints.** We understand their business reality (budget, timeline, politics) and work within it.
- **Accountability.** When things don't go as planned, we own it, fix it, and learn from it.

---

## The Client Relationship Cycle

A healthy client relationship moves through phases:

1. **Onboarding** — Building initial trust and understanding
2. **Execution** — Delivering great work consistently
3. **Growth** — Expanding scope and deepening partnership
4. **Retention** — Preventing drift and staying relevant

### Phase 1: Onboarding (First 90 Days)

**Goal:** Establish trust, prove competence, and become an integrated part of the client's team.

#### Key Activities

**Kickoff Meeting**
See [Project Initiation Playbook](/playbooks/project-initiation-playbook.md) for detailed guidance. The kickoff sets the tone for the entire relationship.

**First Project Checklist**
- Deliver the first project on time or early (not late)
- Exceed expectations on quality
- Communicate proactively about progress and any issues
- Be responsive to client questions and feedback
- Show up as a strategic partner, not just a vendor

**Relationship Building**
- Learn the client's business deeply (their market, competitors, strategy)
- Meet key stakeholders beyond the primary contact
- Understand their decision-making process and who influences it
- Ask about their goals and challenges beyond your current scope
- Share insights about their category or industry (not just your work)

**Establish Communication Norms**
- Agree on meeting frequency and format (weekly check-in? as-needed?)
- Establish how feedback will be given (email? calls? collaborative document?)
- Define escalation paths (if something's wrong, who do we tell and how?)
- Set expectations for response time
- Clarify how urgent vs. non-urgent requests are handled

**First Deliverable Review**
- Prepare thoroughly (no surprises in the meeting)
- Bring strategic context, not just the work
- Be ready to defend decisions
- Listen to feedback without defensiveness
- Ask clarifying questions if feedback is vague
- Follow up in writing: what was approved, what's next, when

#### 90-Day Checkpoint

At 90 days, have a conversation with the client:

- "How's the partnership going from your perspective?"
- "What's working well? What could we improve?"
- "Are we meeting your expectations?"
- "What are your priorities for the next 90 days?"

This shows that you care about their experience, not just the project.

### Phase 2: Execution (Ongoing Work)

**Goal:** Deliver consistently excellent work while building deeper strategic relationships.

#### Delivery Standards

**Meeting Deadlines**
- Your deadline is 24-48 hours before the client's deadline (not the same day)
- If you're going to miss a deadline, tell the client immediately, with a revised timeline and how it affects their plans
- Build buffer into your schedules; use it for quality, not procrastination

**Quality Standards**
- Work should be strategic, creative, and measurable
- Spelling, grammar, and production quality should be flawless
- Deliverables should include context (why we made these choices) and strategic rationale
- Nothing should feel half-baked or rushed

**Proactive Communication**
- Brief the client on progress before they ask
- Flag potential issues early (budget overruns, timeline slips, quality concerns)
- Don't wait for the client to realize there's a problem
- Share learnings and insights as work progresses
- Keep them informed about what's happening behind the scenes

**Managing Revisions**
See "Revision Process" section below for detailed guidance. In brief:
- Be clear about how many rounds are built in
- Understand what feedback means before revising
- Distinguish between revision rounds and scope changes
- Don't just incorporate all feedback blindly—push back thoughtfully when feedback conflicts with strategy

#### Regular Communication

**Weekly Check-Ins**
Hold a brief (30 min) call or email with the primary contact:
- What we're working on this week
- What we need from them (feedback, approvals, information)
- Any issues or questions
- Timeline for next deliverable

This prevents surprises and keeps the relationship connected.

**Monthly Strategic Check-In**
Beyond the current project, have a quarterly or bi-monthly conversation about the bigger picture:
- How's the category performing?
- What are they seeing from competitors?
- What's changing in their business?
- Are there emerging opportunities or challenges?
- How can DE help them think strategically?

This positions you as a strategic partner, not just a project executor.

**Campaign Retrospective**
After each campaign launches, schedule a debrief:
- What was our objective? Did we achieve it?
- What worked well in the creative, media, and execution?
- What would we do differently next time?
- What did we learn about the audience or market?
- How do we apply these learnings to the next campaign?

This shows accountability and continuous improvement.

#### Handling Problems

**When You Mess Up**
1. Tell the client immediately (don't hide it)
2. Explain what happened (honestly, without excuses)
3. Explain how you're fixing it
4. Explain how you'll prevent it next time
5. Don't oversell your solution (be realistic about what it involves)

**When the Client Changes Direction**
1. Understand what's changed and why
2. Clarify how this affects scope, timeline, and budget
3. Present options and tradeoffs
4. Get alignment before starting work
5. Document the change (it's a scope change, not a revision)

**When the Client is Unhappy**
1. Listen without interrupting
2. Understand what they're unhappy about (the work? the process? an outcome?)
3. Don't defend or make excuses
4. Ask what would make it better
5. Work with them to solve it
6. Check in after the fix to make sure they're satisfied

**When You Disagree with the Client**
1. Understand their perspective fully
2. Present your perspective with strategic rationale
3. Present options with tradeoffs
4. Respect their decision (it's their brand)
5. Execute their decision fully, not half-heartedly

### Phase 3: Growth (Expanding the Partnership)

**Goal:** Expand scope and deepen strategic partnership.

#### Signposts of a Healthy Relationship

- Client actively seeks your input on strategy, not just execution
- They include you in planning conversations early
- You're part of their team, not an outside vendor
- They introduce you to other departments or stakeholders
- They trust your judgment and take your recommendations seriously
- They refer you to other potential clients or partners

#### How to Drive Growth

**Bring Strategic Ideas**
Don't just respond to briefs. Bring ideas to the table:
- "We've been thinking about your audience, and here's an insight we think could shape your next campaign..."
- "We've noticed a shift in how your competitors are positioning. Here's how we think you should respond..."
- "There's an emerging opportunity in [channel/demographic]. Should we explore this together?"

**Demonstrate Value Beyond Your Scope**
- Share research or insights from your industry knowledge
- Introduce them to partners or vendors who might help their business
- Recommend other agencies or specialists if the work is outside your wheelhouse (yes, really—it builds trust)
- Offer perspective on their business challenges, even if they're not currently asking for work

**Propose Expansion**
When appropriate:
- "We've been working on your consumer campaigns. Have you thought about B2B or trade marketing?"
- "We're strong on broadcast and digital. Should we also think about PR or influencer partnerships?"
- "What if we invested in ongoing research on your category? We could bring you insights quarterly..."

**Build Relationships Beyond the Primary Contact**
- Meet the CEO or CMO periodically
- Understand other departments' needs and challenges
- Build advocates for your partnership across their organization

#### Growth Conversation

Annually, schedule a strategic business review (SBR) with the client:

**Agenda:**
1. **What we've accomplished together this year** (campaigns, results, impact)
2. **What's changed in their business and market** (new competitors, shifting audience, new opportunities)
3. **What's working well in our partnership** (what should we keep doing?)
4. **What could be better** (honest feedback from both sides)
5. **What's next** (priorities for next year, new opportunities, budget and resource planning)

This is not a sales pitch. It's a strategic conversation about how the partnership is evolving.

### Phase 4: Retention (Staying Relevant)

**Goal:** Prevent drift and keep the partnership strong as market and client priorities change.

#### Drift Happens When

- Communication becomes sporadic
- Work becomes rote (you're just executing, not thinking)
- You're not in the room for strategic decisions
- A new decision-maker comes in and doesn't know the agency
- The client finds someone cheaper or "newer" and starts comparing
- You take the relationship for granted

#### How to Prevent Drift

**Stay Connected**
- Don't disappear between projects
- Proactively share insights even if they're not asking
- Check in on their business, not just your work
- Notice when something changes (new competitor, new product, new market) and bring perspective

**Stay Strategic**
- Keep bringing ideas and thinking to the table
- Don't let work become transactional
- Invest in understanding their business at a deeper level
- Ask "why" even when you think you know the answer

**Stay Flexible**
- Understand when their business needs change (it always does)
- Adapt your approach as their market shifts
- Be willing to pivot or propose new services if their needs evolve
- Don't insist that they need the same thing they needed 2 years ago

**Stay Visible**
- Rotate team members into the relationship (client should know multiple people at DE, not just one person)
- Invite them to industry events, presentations, or dinners
- Include them in your thinking (beta-test ideas with them, ask their perspective)
- Celebrate wins together

**Stay Honest**
- Tell them when something isn't working
- Don't pretend you're the best at everything (refer when appropriate)
- Share learnings, even when they're uncomfortable
- Give honest feedback about their business or market, even if they don't ask

## Key Processes

### The Revision Process

**The Problem:** Unlimited revision cycles destroy budgets, timelines, and morale.

**The Solution:** Clear revision expectations, built into the brief and process.

**How to Handle Revisions**

**Round 1: Exploratory**
- Client has seen the work for the first time
- Feedback might be scattered (different people have different opinions)
- Your job: Listen, understand, identify patterns in the feedback
- Don't revise yet—first clarify what feedback means

**Recommended approach:**
- Let client feedback settle for 24-48 hours
- Meet with client to clarify ambiguous feedback
- Ask questions: "When you say 'more sophisticated,' what does that mean?" "Is the strategic direction off, or just the execution?"
- Synthesize feedback into clear revision direction
- Revise based on the clarified direction

**Round 2: Refinement**
- You've made changes based on clear feedback
- This is about refinement, not reconception
- If the client wants to go in a completely new direction, that's a new concept—not a revision

**Revision vs. New Direction**
Know the difference:

**Revision:** "Make the typography bigger" or "The blue is too bright, try a darker shade" or "Add more personality to the copy"

**New Direction:** "Actually, we want to go a different direction entirely" or "Can we explore a completely different approach?" or "This doesn't feel right—let's start over"

If it's a new direction, that's a scope change. Treat it as such.

**How to Prevent Revision Creep**
1. **Clear brief.** Avoid this problem by having a tight brief that everyone's agreed on
2. **Present with strategy.** When you present work, explain the strategic thinking. This helps clients understand why you made choices, not just react to what they see
3. **Limit rounds in the brief.** "We've built in 2 revision rounds" is clear. Unlimited revisions leads to endless cycles
4. **Push back thoughtfully.** If feedback conflicts with the strategy, say so: "That revision would move us away from the strategy we agreed on. Should we re-examine the strategy, or stick with our original direction?"
5. **Document changes.** Each revision round should have a clear output. Don't let feedback layer on indefinitely

### Managing Scope Creep

**The Problem:** Project scope expands without budget or timeline expanding. Everyone's unhappy.

**The Solution:** Clear scope definition and willingness to name scope changes.

**In the Brief:**
- Explicit list of deliverables
- Explicit list of what's NOT included
- Explicit revision process
- Timeline and approval process

**During Execution:**
- If the client asks for something outside the scope, flag it
- Don't just say yes and absorb the cost
- Ask: "Is this something we should build into the project, or is this a new request?"
- If it's new, discuss options: "We can add this, and here's what that does to timeline/budget. Or we can prioritize and defer it. What makes sense?"

**Common Scope Creep Requests:**
- "Can you also design this application for mobile?" (different from desktop)
- "We need 4 more variations of this ad" (not the same as revision)
- "Can you also develop a media plan?" (usually not in a creative brief)
- "We'd like to include social media content" (often not part of the original scope)

For each: Clarify, discuss tradeoffs, get agreement before proceeding.

### Feedback Interpretation

**The Client Says:** "Make it pop"

**What they might mean:**
- Add more color or contrast
- Make it bigger or bolder
- Make it more energetic or exciting
- Make it more distinctive or memorable
- Add more visual impact

**What you should ask:** "What specifically feels flat? Is it the color, the size, the energy? Can you point to an example of 'pop' that you like?"

**The Client Says:** "It doesn't feel right"

**What they might mean:**
- The tone is off
- The visual direction is wrong
- The strategy doesn't feel right
- They don't like it but can't articulate why
- Something subtle feels misaligned

**What you should ask:** "Help me understand. Is this about the strategy, the tone, the visual style, or something else? Can you tell me what would make it feel 'right'?"

**The Client Says:** "More sophisticated"

**What they might mean:**
- More premium-looking (better typography, more whitespace, higher production)
- More serious or refined (less playful, more elegant)
- More strategic or thoughtful (less obvious or superficial)
- More modern or contemporary

**What you should ask:** "When you say sophisticated, do you mean the visual style (typography, color, imagery) or the overall tone and approach? Can you show me an example?"

**Always clarify before revising.**

## Communication Standards

### Email Communication

**Tone:** Professional but warm. You're partners, not serving a king.

**Structure:**
- Brief greeting and context
- Main point or request
- Supporting detail or explanation
- Clear next steps (what you need, by when)
- Sign-off

**Response Time:** 24 business hours (or set a different standard with the client)

**Example Email:**

*"Hi [Client Name],*

*We finished the first round of concepts for the campaign and wanted to get your thoughts before we move forward. We've attached the files and a brief strategic summary of why we approached it this way.*

*Can you review and send us feedback by [date]? That'll give us time to revise and get back to you by [delivery date].*

*Let me know if you have questions about any of the concepts."*

### Phone & Video Calls

**Always confirm before calling.** "Can I call you at 2pm today?" is better than an unexpected ring.

**Come prepared.** Know what you want to discuss. Have your materials ready.

**Listen more than you talk.** Calls are for dialogue, not monologue.

**Follow up in writing.** After a call, send an email: "Here's what we discussed and agreed to..."

### Meetings

**Have an agenda.** Send it to the client 2-3 days before the meeting.

**Start on time, end on time.** Respect their calendar.

**Take notes.** Someone on the DE team should document decisions and action items.

**Follow up.** Send notes within 24 hours, summarizing what was discussed and what's next.

### Handling Difficult Conversations

**When you need to say no:**
- Be direct and clear
- Explain why (not just "that's outside our scope")
- Offer an alternative if possible
- Don't apologize for having boundaries

Example: "We can add that deliverable, but it would push our timeline to [new date] and would require an additional budget of [amount]. We could also prioritize and do it in a second phase. Which makes sense for you?"

**When you need to give bad news:**
- Tell them immediately
- Explain what happened
- Explain what you're doing about it
- Explain next steps

Example: "We discovered an issue with the production timeline. The vendor has a 2-week delay. We're exploring options with 3 other vendors today. I'll have an update for you by tomorrow with revised timeline and any cost impact."

**When you disagree:**
- Listen fully to their perspective
- State your perspective with reasoning
- Present tradeoffs
- Respect their decision

Example: "I understand your concern about the tone. We chose this direction because [strategic reasoning]. Here's what a softer approach might do to the message. You might be right, and I'm happy to explore it—what would feel better to you?"

## Relationship Metrics

How do you know if the partnership is healthy?

**Green Lights:**
- Client returns calls/emails promptly and proactively
- Client seeks your input on strategy, not just execution
- Client introduces you to new departments or decision-makers
- Client renews contracts or increases budget/scope
- Client refers you to other prospects
- Client provides positive feedback and testimonials
- Client's business is growing and they're investing in marketing

**Yellow Lights:**
- Client is slow to respond or less engaged
- Client communication becomes transactional
- Client starts talking to other agencies for competitive proposals
- Client reduces budget or scope
- New decision-maker arrives and doesn't know the history
- Client gives mixed feedback about your work or partnership

**Red Lights:**
- Client pulls back on budget significantly
- Client explicitly says they're exploring other agencies
- Relationship has moved entirely to a new contact (previous relationships disappear)
- Communication becomes infrequent or difficult
- Client asks for spec work or steep discounts
- You're no longer invited to strategic planning meetings

**If you see yellow or red lights, address it.** Have a conversation about the partnership. Don't wait for the client to leave.

## Annual Relationship Review

Once a year, do a formal review of each major client relationship:

**Account Lead + Account Team Review:**
- How's this relationship performing against our expectations?
- What's working well? What's struggling?
- Is this a growth opportunity or a declining account?
- What's our strategy for the next 12 months?
- Do we have the right team assigned?
- Is the budget/margin appropriate for the work?
- Are there risks (client unhappy, talking to competitors, personnel changes)?

Annually reviewing relationships keeps you proactive instead of reactive.

## Key Principles

- **Relationships are investments.** The first project is about proving competence. The second and third are about deepening partnership. The tenth is about strategic collaboration.
- **Show up as strategic partners.** Think about their business, not just your project.
- **Communicate proactively.** Don't wait for problems to surface. Tell clients what's happening, when things change, and what you're thinking.
- **Deliver consistently.** It's not enough to be great once. You have to be great every time.
- **Stay curious.** Keep learning about their business, their market, their customers, and their challenges.
- **Respect their constraints.** They live with budget, timeline, and political realities you don't. Work within them, don't ignore them.
- **Be honest.** Sometimes that means pushing back. Sometimes that means admitting you were wrong. Always means being truthful.
- **Build teams, not dependencies.** The client should know multiple people at DE. If they only know you, that's a risk to the relationship.
